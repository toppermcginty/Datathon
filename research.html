Ultrasound for Dense Breasts

Women with dense breasts have a higher risk of breast cancer simply due to there being more tissue. This can also make it harder to see potential tumours on a mammogram, as effect called “masking”. For these women, a mammogram alone is not the best option. Because of these limitations, supplemental screening with other tests like whole-breast ultrasound or an MRI is often recommended. Especially for higher-risk patients or those with denser breasts.

Ultrasound-Guided Biopsy

If an imaging test finds a suspicious mass, the next step is typically to perform a biopsy to obtain tissue for a definitive diagnosis. The current preferred method for sampling a suspicious breast mass is a core needle biopsy; this procedure is often performed while using an ultrasound for guidance. Using an ultrasound for guidance allows the radiologist to view the needle in real-time as it enters the mass, ensuring that the tissue samples are taken from the correct location.

Understanding Breast Cancer Risk Factors

Knowing the different risk factors for breast cancer can help your doctor understand your personal risk profile. These factors are divided into things you cannot change and those you can. Non-modifiable factors include things such as getting older, family history or specific genetic mutations as all as having dense breast tissue. Factors that you can change, including lifestyle choices. Your risk level can be influenced by not being physically active, drinking alcohol, and smoking.

Selection Biases

Selection bias can occur when data used in breast cancer research comes from only one region or location. If studies collect information from a single geographic area, the results may not represent the broader population. For example, healthcare access, environmental exposures, and population characteristics may differ in other regions, which could affect breast cancer risk or outcomes. Similarly, if all screening tests are conducted at the same hospital or using the same imaging machine, the results may reflect the performance of that specific setting rather than the healthcare system. This can limit how well the findings apply to other hospitals, technologies, or populations.

Lifestyle Factors

Another limitation in breast cancer datasets is the lack of information about lifestyle factors. Behaviours such as smoking, alcohol consumption, diet, and levels of physical activity can influence the risk of developing breast cancer. If these factors are not included in the dataset, models or research findings may overlook important contributors to the disease. Different populations often have different lifestyle patterns, and failing to account for these differences can reduce the accuracy of predictions and risk assessments. As a result, the data may not fully capture the complexity of breast cancer risk across different groups of people.
